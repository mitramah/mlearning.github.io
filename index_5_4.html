Language-Learning Software--Babbel or Duolingo

Today's language learners have little difficulty finding a free language lesson taught by a language app that can be purchased from the mobile app store. These language apps provide highly personalized, bite-sized lessons that are perfect for learning on the go or whenever there is an opening in the learners' schedule. The progress of the learners can be traced across devices or compared with their friends' progress via social media such as Facebook; a successful use of "gamification".

Convenience is another feature that is specifically mentioned by the users of these language apps. The modern learners might come across the confusion in their choice of a right language app. While there are thousands of apps available among current app stores, the most popular ones are Duolingo and Babbel with millions of registered users. Babble claims to have 1300 new users registered every hour, signing up for the free trial. Duolingo is a free language-learning app with 110 million users worldwide. It is claimed to be the most downloaded app in the education category on both Google Play Store and iTunes. Both apps are accessible via smartphones and desktop computers, but the app version renders the 'anytime, anywhere' learning expected from a mobile learning software.

Duolingo uses a task-based, gamified, multiple choice format to build the language learner's confidence in using the language. Duolingo’s speaking quizzes utilize the voice recognition tech which makes the learning process gratifying. The languages are available to the learners based on the players' mother tongue: if Spanish, they can choose from English, Portuguese, and French (and soon, German), compared with more than ten languages if the players speak English. Machine learning as the back-end technology automatically adjusts the difficulty level depending on the performance in the lessons which range from listening and speaking to multiple choice tests.

Babbel’s lessons, unlike Duolingo’s, first focus on building basic conversational skills. According to Gunter et al (2016), Babbel is rather a learning app, as it is composed of a set of language lessons that do not present any gamification.

For students learning English, Spanish, French, Portuguese, German or Italian, Duolingo and Babbel might work quite well together, filling in the other’s gaps. I, personally, plan to continue using both. The competition between language-learning software development companies can only result in better developments in free and low-cost services the years to come.
