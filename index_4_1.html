Mobile Learning & MALL Apps

Language learning has been on the forefront of the mobile learning evolution, overtaking the proliferation of personal computers by an abundance of free and affordable mobile apps in dedicated online stores from Apple, Android, BlackBerry, Windows Mobile and others. There are more than 80,000 apps on the market, around 30,000 of which are devoted to language learning, especially English. Most of them have been developed outside the academic world (Vermeulen & Ibáñez Moreno, 2015). Language learning has also been revitalized by significant global demand from developing economies where language learning plays a role in the improvement of employment and financial growth (Díaz-Vera, 2012). A taxi driver in India, for instance, might benefit from the time he's waiting in a taxi line to use a mobile device to acquire conversational skills in English that can improve his earnings through better tips. This example highlights the opportunity of convergence of time and place, with an impulse to focus on available learning content and interaction to the learner's need (ibid, p:6).

According to Traxler (2010), mobile learning is in its golden era and it has no intention to stop advancing. This phenomenon uncovers the power of the ICTs and of mobile devices shortly, which will not only be a support for learning but will also change our way of learning:

“ Mobile devices will soon support every pedagogic option including the didactic and the discursive, the individual learning and the social (…). Mobile devices affect many aspects of the process by which knowledge, ideas, images, information and, hence, learning material is produced, stored, distributed, delivered and consumed. ”

Some features are exclusively affiliated to the field of mobile learning: personalized learning, based on user-generated contexts. Thus, as previously mentioned here, mobile learning utilizes more various and flexible types of technology such as personal digital assistants (PDAs), smartphones, iPads, iPods, netbooks, media players, etc.

The first mobile learning app was introduced in 2008 in App stores for both Apple and Android devices, enabling the informal learning as a possibility to be implemented “24 hours a day, seven days a week". It allows mobility at five levels: mobility in the physical space, technological mobility, mobility in the conceptual space, mobility in the social space and decentralized learning (Sharples et al., 2007) ”. The focus of specialized mobile learning, MALL is referred to the use of mobile technology in language learning. Apart from lifelong learning and the acquisition of the ICT skills in all areas of education, today's language teachers are the teachers of the language of global communication. While communication is increasingly digitally mediated, the learners need the digital skills to be fully functional citizens in the 21st century. Therefore, the language teachers should promote these skills in parallel with teaching English (teachingenglish.org).

The emergence of mobile learning in the field of language learning was launched with the first MALL app introduced by the British Council in 2009 (Vermeulen & Ibáñez Moreno, 2015). In this sense, the term MALL is closely linked to the progress and development that mobile learning has experienced in the last decade as well as the wide variety of mobile devices, and users included, in this growing world. Thus, research on language learning and teaching should be adapted to the specific new needs of our modern society (e.g. mobility). Accordingly, mobile-assisted language learning (MALL) activities should be app-based; and “this is not a trend language educators can ignore” (Godwin-Jones, 2011, p. 8).
