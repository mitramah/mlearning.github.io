Introduction

In today's college campuses, students are carrying small and portable mobile devices in their hands instead of a backpack full of books. It can be observed that students carry mobile phones everywhere around the campus. In the hall, they are busy watching videos on their mobile device, or typing messages to their friends or relatives. Students also send and receive videos to their friends in any case either it is for learning or entertainment. Naismith, et. al. (2004), regards the use of mobile technologies in our everyday life in the following way:

“ We take it for granted that we can talk to other people at any time, from wherever we may be; we are beginning to see it as normal that we access information, take photographs, record our thoughts on the device, and that we can share these with our friends, colleagues or the wider world. ”

The concurrent trend of an increase in capabilities with a decrease in price, has led to the ubiquitous nature of mobile devices. Currently there are 7.33 billion mobile subscriptions globally, which is about 99.7 percent of the worldâ€™s population (International Telecommunication Union, 2016). Beyond any doubt, mobile devices are changing the way we live, work, and socialize. From our mobile devices we can instantly access our emails, read articles, interact with others through social networking and SMS, buy clothes, order food, and even check into a flight at an airport using the boarding app. Mobile devices provide users with the flexibility to perform a variety of tasks on a single device that once took several avenues to accomplish (Pollara, 2011). But, how are mobile devices changing the way we teach and learn?

Today's students, who Prensky (2001) called "digital natives" are direct in conflict with their "digital immigrants" -- teachers. Prensky believes that students in the " digital age of the 21st century", have a different experience and thinking pattern which leads to a significantly different brain structure. Thus, the educators need to implement different approaches and rethink current pedagogical strategies. Today's students prefer the moderate use of Information and Communication Technologies (ICT), as well as multimedia in their courses. The students demand more flexibility, and alternative modes of delivery of instruction. In addition, universities should implement the changes in teaching and learning strategies that are well conceived, well explained, and properly embedded in courses (Jones et al., 2011).

Language learning is also becoming easier with the introduction of various mobile devices, specifically, mobile phones. Integrating mobile learning into the learning environment, is just one potential way to meet the needs of both students and universities in the digital age of the 21st century. This study seeks to investigate methods of effective learning through mobile technologies, which signifying a shift from teacher-led learning to student-led learning, via m-learning. The possibilities of learning a second language in a mobile-based environment are demonstrated, and are accompanied by some examples of mobile applications that assist language learners with their language acquisition.
