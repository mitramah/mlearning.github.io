Social Impacts of MALL & Web 2.0

The development of mobile technologies has created different personal, cultural and social aspects that differ from desktop technologies. Interacting with a desktop computer occurs in a bubble, at a predetermined time and place where the learner is disconnected from the rest of the world for a substantial and premeditated period of time. On the contrary, interacting with mobile technologies is different and is woven into all the times and places of students’ lives. Mobile phones have created “simultaneity of place”: “a physical space and a virtual space of conversational interaction, and an extension of physical space, through the creation and juxtaposition of a mobile social space” (Traxler, 2010). Mobile devices decimate the demand to associate particular activities to particular places or particular times. They are reconstructing the existing relationships between public and private spaces, and creating the structure in which these relationships are penetrated by virtual spaces. With the emergence of mobile technologies, the static virtual communities and discussions previously bound by networked PCs have been transformed into more fluid environments. The private sphere “is no longer conceivable as what goes on, discreetly, in the life of the individual away from the public domain, or as subsequently represented in individual consciousness” (Cooper 2002 cited in Traxler 2010).

The educational theories of social constructivism (Vygotsky 1978) and connectivism (Siemens 2004) place emphasis on the influence of the social context, and how one constructs their learning (e.g. burgeoning uses of social web or Web 2.0 tools), and the connectedness of knowledge and learners. In this context, the learners share their potential life experience and knowledge which transforms a traditional teacher-centered learning setting where learners are the resource for interpreting, challenging, and creating new knowledge (Low and O’Connell, 2006).

Due to the robust mobile technology infrastructure in communication and internet connections, learners can take advantage of both formal and informal learning methods. The development of Web 2.0 and many mobile applications has resulted in the proliferation of social networking. Some researchers argue that Web 2.0 tools work in harmony with social learning institutions (Bicen, 2015). The additional studies suggest that Web 2.0 tools and mobile applications provide opportunities for creating interactive learning environments (Kim et al., 09). For instance, “Use of Web 2.0 tools, like wiki pages, can allow students to collaborate through a multitude of media” (Prince, 2012).

Mobile technologies can facilitate a social practice approach “in which the learner uses a variety of locations to enact and rehearse a personal voice” (Díaz-Vera, 2012). The convergence of mobile technologies with the wider user generated movements associated with Web 2.0 software, promotes the web as a medium for writing and alters the passive, and read-only participation. It uses technologies such as wikis, mashups, blogs, news feeds and podcasts to move the web from a centralized broadcast medium to one where everyone has a voice. Mobile students now have a voice because they can create – for example, images, sounds, data and voices themselves – and share it not only with the outside but the inside world. That is to say; they can exchange information about themselves, their friends and affiliations, their feelings, their days and their doings with others outside of their circle (Traxler, 2010).

Within the scope of language learning, researchers have compared the impacts of different technologies on language learning, specifically second language learning. In their recent findings, Web 2.0 technologies seem to have expanded the scope of inquiry on technology and language learning beyond the traditional four major language skills. Web 2.0 learning environments have offered new contexts which new literacies, new genres, new identities, and new pedagogies can be investigated (Wang & Vasquez, 2010).
